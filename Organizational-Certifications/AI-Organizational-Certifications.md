# Organizational AI Certifications and Assurance Programs

This page compares organization-level certifications, assurance designations, registry programs, and supporting standards for demonstrating the secure, safe, responsible, and governed use of artificial intelligence.

> **Status:** Reviewed on July 13, 2026. Program requirements, validation mechanisms, accreditation arrangements, and availability may change. Organizations should confirm the current requirements with the scheme owner, certification body, and accreditation body before beginning an assessment.

## Executive summary

The two leading organization-level pathways covered on this page are:

1. **ISO/IEC 42001:2023 certification** for an independently audited Artificial Intelligence Management System.
2. **Cloud Security Alliance STAR for AI** for transparent, registry-based AI security, safety, governance, and operational assurance.

The strongest combined pathway is:

> **Accredited ISO/IEC 42001 certification + CSA STAR for AI Level 2**

ISO/IEC 42001 provides the auditable AI management-system foundation. STAR for AI Level 2 adds CSA's AI Controls Matrix, validated AI-CAIQ evidence, registry transparency, and an AI-specific assurance designation.

## ISO/IEC 42001 organizational certification

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 specifies requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an **Artificial Intelligence Management System**, or AIMS. It is applicable to organizations of any size that develop, provide, procure, deploy, or use AI-based products or services.

### Core assurance areas

| Area | Organizational expectation |
|---|---|
| Governance | Defined AI policy, leadership accountability, roles, responsibilities, objectives, and oversight |
| Risk management | Identification, analysis, evaluation, treatment, monitoring, and review of AI-related risks and opportunities |
| AI impact assessment | Assessment of potential effects on individuals, groups, society, operations, rights, safety, and other interested parties |
| Lifecycle governance | Controls covering design, data, development, acquisition, deployment, operation, monitoring, change, and retirement |
| Data governance | Data quality, provenance, preparation, suitability, access, privacy, security, and lifecycle controls |
| Human oversight | Defined authority, review, intervention, escalation, and accountability mechanisms |
| Supplier management | Governance of external AI services, models, data, platforms, dependencies, and contractual responsibilities |
| Transparency and traceability | Documented decisions, system information, intended use, limitations, logs, monitoring, and evidence |
| Assurance and improvement | Metrics, monitoring, internal audit, management review, nonconformity management, corrective action, and continual improvement |

### Certification and accreditation

ISO develops the standard but **does not certify organizations or issue ISO certificates**. Certification is performed by external certification bodies. Accreditation provides independent recognition that a certification body is competent to perform the relevant certification activity.

Organizations should verify:

1. The exact legal entity, locations, services, AI systems, and activities included in the certificate's scope.
2. The certification body's competence and accreditation status.
3. Whether the accreditation scope specifically covers ISO/IEC 42001 certification.
4. The certificate number, validity period, surveillance cycle, and current status.
5. Any exclusions, boundaries, outsourced processes, or shared-responsibility dependencies.

### ISO/IEC 42006:2025

[ISO/IEC 42006:2025](https://www.iso.org/standard/42006) establishes additional requirements for bodies that audit and certify AIMS against ISO/IEC 42001. It supplements ISO/IEC 17021-1 with AI-specific competence and audit requirements intended to strengthen consistency, credibility, and confidence in AIMS certification.

## Cloud Security Alliance STAR for AI

CSA STAR for AI extends the Security, Trust, Assurance, and Risk program to AI services. It combines CSA's AI Controls Matrix, AI-CAIQ evidence, registry transparency, validation mechanisms, and ISO/IEC 42001 certification.

### STAR for AI assurance levels

| Level | Validation model | Main requirement | Appropriate interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **AI Trustworthy Pledge** | Public commitment | Adoption of high-level AI trust and safety principles | A commitment signal, not certification or control validation |
| **Level 1** | Self-assessment | Complete and submit the AI-CAIQ to the STAR Registry | Transparent organizational assertion of control practices |
| **Level 1, Valid-AI-ted** | Automated validation of self-assessment | AI-CAIQ passes CSA's Valid-AI-ted system | Enhanced self-assessment with structured and explainable scoring |
| **Level 2** | Third-party certification plus validated self-assessment | ISO/IEC 42001 certification and Valid-AI-ted AI-CAIQ | Advanced organizational assurance combining independent certification and registry transparency |

### AI Controls Matrix and AI-CAIQ

The [AI Controls Matrix](https://cloudsecurityalliance.org/artifacts/ai-controls-matrix) provides controls for developing, deploying, and operating AI systems responsibly. CSA maps the AICM to major standards and frameworks, including ISO/IEC 42001, ISO/IEC 27001, and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework.

The AI-CAIQ translates AICM expectations into a structured questionnaire that organizations use to disclose and assess their AI control environment. It supports customer due diligence, supplier assurance, gap analysis, evidence collection, and STAR for AI submissions.

## Relationship between ISO/IEC 42001 and STAR for AI

| Dimension | ISO/IEC 42001 certification | CSA STAR for AI Level 2 |
|---|---|---|
| Primary purpose | Certify an organizational AI management system against an international standard | Demonstrate AI assurance through ISO/IEC 42001, AICM-aligned evidence, validation, and registry transparency |
| Assessment basis | ISO/IEC 42001 requirements and the defined AIMS scope | ISO/IEC 42001 certificate plus Valid-AI-ted AI-CAIQ |
| Scheme structure | Management-system certification | Composite assurance designation and public registry program |
| Independent assurance | External certification-body audit | Inherited from the required ISO/IEC 42001 certification |
| Public transparency | Certificate and scope statement, subject to organizational disclosure | STAR Registry entry and structured control information |
| Control granularity | Organization-wide management-system requirements and risk-based controls | AICM and AI-CAIQ control-level evidence layered onto the AIMS certification |
| Recommended role | Governance and assurance foundation | Advanced market-facing and customer-facing AI assurance layer |

## Supporting organizational certifications

The following certifications can strengthen the control environment around an AI program but should not be presented as substitutes for AI-specific assurance:

| Supporting certification | Contribution to AI assurance | Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| **ISO/IEC 27001** | Information-security governance, risk treatment, access control, incident management, supplier security, and secure operations | Does not independently demonstrate comprehensive AI governance, impact assessment, or responsible-AI lifecycle management |
| **ISO/IEC 27701** | Privacy information management for personal data used across AI development and operation | Does not independently cover the complete AI safety, governance, or model-risk environment |
| **ISO 9001** | Quality management, process control, customer focus, corrective action, and continual improvement | Not AI-specific and does not replace AI risk or impact assessment |
| **CSA STAR for cloud services** | Cloud-service security assurance using CSA's Cloud Controls Matrix | Focuses on cloud assurance and should not be confused with STAR for AI |
| **SOC 2 reporting** | Independent reporting on controls relevant to security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, or privacy | Scope and criteria vary; a SOC 2 report is not an AI management-system certification |

## Recommended organizational pathway

### Stage 1: Define scope and ownership

1. Establish executive accountability and an AI governance committee.
2. Inventory AI systems, models, agents, data sources, third-party services, and business uses.
3. Define the intended AIMS and certification scope.
4. Identify regulatory, contractual, safety, security, privacy, and ethical obligations.

### Stage 2: Build the management system

1. Implement ISO/IEC 42001 governance and lifecycle processes.
2. Perform AI risk and impact assessments.
3. Map controls to the CSA AICM and other applicable frameworks.
4. Establish documentation, evidence ownership, monitoring, metrics, incident response, and corrective action.

### Stage 3: Establish transparent assurance

1. Complete the AI-CAIQ.
2. Use STAR for AI Level 1 as an initial transparency milestone.
3. Resolve evidence and control gaps identified through validation or internal assurance.

### Stage 4: Obtain independent certification

1. Select a competent certification body and verify accreditation.
2. Complete readiness review, Stage 1 audit, corrective actions, and Stage 2 audit.
3. Verify the final certificate scope and public claims.

### Stage 5: Pursue STAR for AI Level 2

1. Maintain a valid ISO/IEC 42001 certification.
2. Submit a Valid-AI-ted AI-CAIQ.
3. Complete the STAR Registry submission and maintain current assurance evidence.
4. Integrate surveillance audits, material AI changes, incidents, and control updates into continual improvement.

## What should not be described as organizational certification

The following are important frameworks, standards, or knowledge bases, but they are not organization-level certifications by themselves:

- NIST AI Risk Management Framework.
- NIST Generative AI Profile.
- ISO/IEC 23894 AI risk-management guidance.
- OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications.
- OWASP Agentic AI guidance.
- MITRE ATLAS.
- Google Secure AI Framework.
- AI safety pledges without independent assessment.
- Internal declarations of compliance without defined assessment criteria and evidence.

## Verification checklist for buyers and assessors

Before relying on an organizational AI certification or designation, verify:

1. **Scheme owner:** Who defines and governs the assurance program?
2. **Assessment type:** Self-assessment, automated validation, independent attestation, or accredited certification?
3. **Scope:** Which entities, locations, services, AI systems, and lifecycle activities are covered?
4. **Criteria:** Which standard, control matrix, questionnaire, or regulatory requirements were assessed?
5. **Assessor competence:** Is the assessor qualified and independent?
6. **Accreditation:** Is the certification body accredited for the relevant standard and scope?
7. **Evidence date:** When was the assessment performed, and is the assurance still valid?
8. **Exceptions:** What exclusions, nonconformities, qualifications, or limitations apply?
9. **Continuous assurance:** How are system changes, model updates, incidents, suppliers, and emerging risks handled?
10. **Public verification:** Can the certificate or registry entry be independently verified?
